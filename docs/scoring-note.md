# Scoring Note

The system ranks jobs with a deterministic 100-point scoring model built around the user’s own job-search profile. The profile captures six inputs: primary job family, seniority level, years of experience, compensation floor, preferred company stage, and career priority (`learning`, `ownership & scope`, or `balanced`). Every job is first normalized into structured attributes, and then scored against that profile. I used five criteria because they best reflect how job seekers actually make decisions. **Job family fit (40 points)** is weighted most heavily because it is the strongest signal of whether a role is even in the right lane; a strategy and operations candidate should not see unrelated jobs ranking near the top. **Level fit (25 points)** combines title seniority and years-of-experience requirements, since a strong family match is still a poor fit if the role is clearly too junior or too senior. **Career value fit (15 points)** adjusts ranking based on whether the user prioritizes learning or ownership; this is what makes a fresh graduate and a ten-year professional rank the same role differently. **Compensation fit (10 points)** checks whether known pay meets the user’s floor, but is weighted modestly because salary data is often incomplete in public job feeds. **Company stage fit (10 points)** rewards alignment with preferences such as startup, growth-stage, late-stage, or public company.

I separated hard constraints from scoring on purpose. Location, remote policy, maximum years required, minimum compensation, allowed seniority, allowed company stage, and “hide unknown salary” are handled as hard filters in the UI rather than as score penalties. That design makes the results easier to reason about: a role that fails a hard requirement should disappear from the list instead of just receiving a slightly lower score.

I excluded LLM-based ranking from the live scoring path. The current system uses deterministic scoring from cached job attributes because it is faster, cheaper, and more explainable when the user edits their profile. I also excluded jobs that do not match any supported role family at ingestion, so irrelevant listings never enter the ranked corpus. Finally, I did not make domain match a primary scoring criterion, because for this product the user’s target job family, level, compensation, company stage, and desired career value are more important than whether the company happens to be in a preferred sector.
